# Layering Guide

The classic three-layer system works for most conditions between the desert and the arctic. Here's how Alpine Forge gear fits together.

## Base layer — manage moisture

Against your skin, wear something that moves sweat away without holding odor. The Glacier Merino Base Layer (200gsm) is the right weight for three-season use: warm enough for belayed mornings, breathable enough for uphill efforts.

For high-output summer activity, skip the base layer or wear a synthetic t-shirt.

## Mid layer — add warmth

Between the base and the shell, add insulation sized to expected temperatures. The Ember Down Hoodie 800FP packs down small and adds a lot of warmth for the weight — ideal for shoulder-season mountaineering or sitting at camp. For active warmth that breathes better when you're moving, pair the Glacier Merino Base Layer with a fleece instead (Alpine Forge doesn't currently make a fleece; we recommend buying third-party).

## Shell — block wind and water

The outer layer manages exposure. Pick based on what you expect:

- **Hardshell** (Summit Shell Jacket, Torrent Rain Pants): driving rain, wet snow, high wind. 3L fabric is durable enough for alpine rock and brush.
- **Wind shell** (Zephyr Cycling Windbreaker): high-output movement in cold dry wind, when a hardshell would overheat.

## Accessories

- **Hands**: Tundra Insulated Mittens for sub-freezing; nothing for summer.
- **Head**: helmet (Headwall Climbing Helmet) if rockfall, hood of the Summit Shell Jacket otherwise.

## Common mistakes

1. **Over-insulating at the start** — you should feel a little cold for the first 5 minutes of moving. If you're warm at the trailhead you'll be soaked an hour later.
2. **Cotton anywhere** — even the underwear. Cotton holds moisture, so in cold conditions it's an active liability.
3. **Shell always on** — the shell is the *exposure* layer. Take it off as soon as exposure drops.
